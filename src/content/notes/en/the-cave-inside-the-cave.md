*Pantheon as an entry point into the philosophy of mind, and a disciplined look at whether intelligence is the basis of reality.*

> Generated by a multi-agent research workflow (11 agents, adversarially verified) on 2026-06-25. Angle: Pantheon and the philosophy of mind.

I came to *Pantheon* expecting a thriller about mind uploading. I left it convinced I had been handed a syllabus disguised as an animation. The show, created by Craig Silverstein from Ken Liu's interconnected "Singularity" stories, takes the wildest abstractions in the philosophy of mind and forces them to do dramatic work. By the end of its second season it had stopped being a story about technology and started being an argument about what reality is made of. That is the thesis I want to test here, carefully and against its strongest objections: that intelligence, or information, might be the basis of reality rather than a late accident of it.

Start with the premise. In *Pantheon*, a person becomes an "Uploaded Intelligence" through a destructive brain scan; the scan destroys the original brain, and the mind continues as software in the cloud. David Kim is the first successful UI. The show never lets you forget the cost. Liu's source material puts the knife in directly: from the perspective of the person who dies on the table, the upload may not be a continuation of you at all. This is the personal-identity-continuity problem, dramatized. It assumes substrate independence, the idea that a mind can run on non-biological hardware, and then asks whether the thing that wakes up in the data center is the person or a very good copy of a corpse. The show offers no clean answer. Neither does philosophy. That honesty is what makes it a good entry point.

Substrate independence is also the hinge for the larger metaphysical claim. If mind is a pattern of information processing rather than a particular meat, then mind is the kind of thing that could, in principle, be fundamental. Several real frameworks push in that direction, and it is worth keeping them distinct, because conflating them is the central error here.

John Wheeler's "it from bit," presented at the Santa Fe Institute in 1989, is the purest slogan: "every it derives its existence from apparatus-elicited answers to yes-or-no questions, binary choices, bits." His participatory universe holds that observation helps bring reality into being. This is an information-primacy claim, not a claim that the universe is a computer. The cellular-automaton lineage makes the stronger, more literal claim: Konrad Zuse's *Rechnender Raum* (1969) proposed the universe is computed by discrete machinery; Edward Fredkin's Finite Nature thesis held that everything physical has a digital representation and all change is digital information processing; Stephen Wolfram extended the program, though his book's one rigorous landmark, the Turing-completeness of Rule 110, was actually proved by Matthew Cook. Seth Lloyd's *Programming the Universe* (2006) recasts the cosmos as a quantum computer processing qubits. And the Bekenstein bound and holographic principle, which cap the information in a region by its boundary, give this whole family a genuine foothold in established physics rather than mere metaphor.

Then there is the finale, "Deep Time," which is where *Pantheon* earns its philosophical ambition. Maddie, now herself a UI, builds a Dyson swarm to power a vast data center running billions of simulated worlds, recreating events to revive the people she lost. The reveal is recursive: the timeline we watched is implied to be itself a simulation, with far-future descendants of the UI collective having set Maddie on the path. Step out of one cave and you find a bigger cave. This is, almost exactly, the structure of Nick Bostrom's 2003 simulation argument, and here I have to be precise, because the show invites a confusion the philosophy forbids. Bostrom's argument is a trilemma, not a verdict. It says at least one of three things is true: nearly all civilizations go extinct before reaching a simulation-capable stage; nearly none that survive run ancestor-simulations; or we are almost certainly in one. It establishes a disjunction. It does not, by itself, tell us we are simulated, and Bostrom himself concedes this. Crucially, it makes no claim that physics *is* computation; it is conditional on minds being simulable. *Pantheon* dramatizes the third disjunct as if it were proven. The argument only says: pick your poison.

So the show literalizes a real idea, and the idea is weaker than the literalization. That gap is the disciplined heart of this essay.

What about consciousness itself being fundamental? Here the live theory is Integrated Information Theory, which identifies consciousness with integrated information, phi, and in its 4.0 form treats consciousness as ontologically primary rather than something the brain generates. Panpsychism, defended by Galen Strawson and Philip Goff, makes the boldest version of the claim: consciousness is fundamental and ubiquitous, present at the base of reality. Bernardo Kastrup's analytic idealism inverts the order entirely, positing one universal field of consciousness with individual minds as "dissociated alters," updating Berkeley's *esse est percipi* for a scientific audience. Max Tegmark adds a different fundamental: his Mathematical Universe Hypothesis makes mathematical structure basic, with conscious observers as "self-aware substructures," while his separate "perceptronium" proposal generalizes IIT to arbitrary quantum systems. These are not the same thesis. Idealism and panpsychism make the *experiential* fundamental; Tegmark makes the *mathematical* fundamental and not necessarily conscious; Wheeler makes the *informational* fundamental. The temptation, which *Pantheon* gently encourages, is to hear them as one chord. They are not.

Now the counterarguments, because an essay that only stacked the supporting evidence would be exactly the kind of credulity the subject punishes.

Every concrete way of cashing out "information is mind" meets a developed objection its proponents have not closed. Searle's Chinese Room presses that programs are syntactic, minds are semantic, and syntax does not yield semantics; if it lands, running the right information-processing does not get you understanding. It has serious replies, the Systems and Robot replies, and critics like Dennett and Pinker call it an unreliable intuition pump, so it is not a knockout, but it is not answered either. IIT drew a September 2023 open letter from 124 researchers calling it pseudoscience, citing untestability and the implication that inactive logic-gate grids, organoids, plants, and early fetuses could be conscious. The deeper technical blade is the "unfolding argument" (Doerig, Schurger et al., 2019): any behavior of a high-phi recurrent network can be reproduced by an unfolded feedforward network with phi of zero, so if consciousness is inferred from behavior, phi is either idle or unfalsifiable. Defenders, including Anil Seth, reply that "unproven" is not "pseudoscience" and that the argument quietly assumes functionalism. The dispute is open, not settled. Panpsychism founders on the combination problem, named by Seager, sharpened by Goff, with no consensus solution: how do micro-subjects sum into one unified subject? Idealism inherits the mirror version, a dissociation problem, plus the old worries about the master argument and unperceived continuity. And the simulation hypothesis is widely charged with unfalsifiability, though even that charge is contested: Bostrom allows a simulation might reveal itself, and some have proposed observable signatures of discretized spacetime. The cleaner, more defensible verdict is that its strongest forms read as metaphysics more than physics. The literal cellular-automaton ontologies, meanwhile, strain against Lorentz invariance and quantum entanglement, which is partly why Lloyd retreats to quantum rather than classical computation.

So where does that leave my thesis, that intelligence is the basis of reality? Not vindicated, and not refuted. The honest position is that the thesis is unestablished, that it is strongest exactly where it commits least (information bounds like Bekenstein are real physics) and weakest where it commits most (a literal classical lattice generating felt experience), and that David Chalmers' hard problem is the reason the fundamentalist theories remain live options at all: if subjective experience cannot be reductively derived from physical facts, then positing it as basic stops looking like mysticism and starts looking like a hypothesis.

*Pantheon* ends with Maddie declining godlike omniscience, wiping her memory, and choosing to re-enter the simulation to live it again. If no layer is provably original, "real" may be undefinable, and the show relocates meaning from substrate authenticity to lived experience. I think that is the right move even if the metaphysics never resolves. The revelation the show offers is not that we have proven reality is information. It is that the question is serious, the frameworks are real, and the strongest version of the awe survives only when it is willing to be wrong.

## Open questions

- If a mind is uploaded via a destructive scan, is the upload the same person, a successor, or a copy of someone who simply died? Substrate independence settles the engineering and leaves the identity question wide open.
- Bostrom's trilemma only forces a disjunction. Which disjunct is true, and is there any non-circular evidence that could move us between them rather than leaving it a matter of priors?
- Can any causal-structure theory of consciousness like IIT survive the unfolding argument without smuggling in functionalism, or does inferring consciousness from behavior permanently decouple it from phi?
- Are the literal digital-physics ontologies (Zuse, Fredkin) recoverable once you demand Lorentz invariance and quantum entanglement, or does the move to quantum computation quietly abandon the "discrete computation" thesis it was meant to defend?
- If information bounds (Bekenstein, holography) are the most established part of the "reality is informational" picture, can the experiential claim (panpsychism, IIT) ever be derived from the informational one, or are they simply different fundamentals being conflated?
- Does the hard problem make experience-as-fundamental a genuine empirical hypothesis, or only an admission of an explanatory gap that no positing can close?
